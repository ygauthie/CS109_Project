About


This is the result of a project we did part of a data science course we took at Harvard during the Fall of 2015. Data science and machine learning play an increasing role in applied finance. Financial markets produce huge and ever increasing volumes of data, which calls for novel methods to discover and explore patterns, and possibly exploit them.


Beating the market, however, is challenging. Countless methods have been proposed to do so. In general, they are not more profitable than the "buy-and-hold" strategy, especially when transaction costs are considered. Nevertheless, we decided to give it a try!

Our approach

  • 1. Market sector selection

    Instead of trying to predict stock prices of specific companies, we try to project the trend of Exchange Traded Funds (ETFs) tracking specific sectors of the U.S. economy. The nine sectors we focus on are real estate, technology, energy, industrials, financials, healthcare, home building, materials, and telecommunications.

  • 2. Technical analysis

    Using historical data from Yahoo! Finance, we compute various technical indicators related to our sectors / ETFs of interest, as well as indicators related to the broader stock market (S&P500). We use these indicators (some quantitative, some binary) as features to feed our machine learning models.

  • 3. Sentiment analysis

    For selected sectors, we conduct natural language analysis of material published in the New York Times. For every trading day, we assess the general sentiment as positive or negative on a 0 to 100 scale. We use this as an additional feature in our models.

  • 4. Predictions

    We train, optimize, validate, and test an ensemble of five machine learning classifiers: logistic regression, support vector machine, random forest, extra trees, and naive Bayes classifiers. We then use the ensemble to predict if a market sector will begin trending up (+) from a trough or down (-) from a peak in the near future. We don't make predictions on the next trading day because we want predictions that are less volatile and won't require making hundreds of trades per year.

  • 5. Trading simulation

    We calculate the profit (or loss) we could have made if we invested 10,000$ in an indexed fund on 01 Jan 2015 and kept it until 01 Dec 2015 (buy-and-hold strategy). This is our sector benchmark. We compare it to how much could have been made by following predictions of our ensemble model, taking into account typical transaction fees of online brokers. We stay in in the market as long as our model tells us to "buy", and out of the market when we get a "sell" signal.

Conclusions

Our predictions are better than random

Our models were accurate between 62% and 75% of the time. That's how often they correctly predicted if an index fund would beging trending up or down in the near future. While not always the best, our ensemble model generally was more accurate than the individual classifiers compositing it, and tended to provide more consistent results across investment sectors.

We beat (most) indices

Trading based on predictions of our ensemble model (i.e., being in the market after a "buy" signal and out after a "sell" signal) over the test period would have produced ROIs superior to the index funds in all but one sector (real estate). For the home construction sector, our model outperformed the index ROI by 15% !

Beating the index isn't sufficient

Choosing the right market sector to invest in is critical. Despite some good performance, our model's ROI would have been negative in three sectors that performed badly in 2015 (real estate, materials, and energy).

Timing is important

Profits are not only a function of the models' accuracies. Some models were more accurate than others but did not return as much in profit because they made wrong predicitions before big market swings. You can be 99% accurate, making the wrong call just before a market crash will severely reduce gains.

We don't predict market downturns well

For instance, most of our models gave a "buy" signal right before the market plunge of August 2015. Had the market plunged even more, most of our gains could have been wiped out.

We're still not rich

Unfortunatley, we didn't trade real money. But our models are certainly promising and apparently deliver better-than-average returns. We will continue to improve upon them and will likely use them to inform our personal investments.
